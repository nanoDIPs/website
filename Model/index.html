Modelling Drug Delivery

Our novel approach to targeted drug delivery has been investigated further by simulating a region of a capillary and surrounding tissue using a system of partial differential equations (PDEs). Through making reasonable assumptions and simplifications of the modelled system, solutions were generated to describe the behaviour of the drug-carrying liposomes when they reach a region where cancerous cells are present in the tissue.

Kinetics of the Reactions

The kinetics of this strand-displacement reaction was based on the methods described by 'Control of DNA Strand Displacement Kinetics using Toehold Exchange' (Winfree & Zhang 2008). The simulations were carried out for two different example toeholds we could use. These are:

TH-1: AGCAAGAAGAGTGAG
TH-3: TAGTACGAGAC

Greek letters are used to denote a domain (a group of consecutive nucleotides) and a bar above indicates the complement to that domain. Furthermore, a domain can be split further into superscripts and subscripts. A superscript of n indicates the n bases that are closes to the 5' part of the domain and subscript of n indicates all the other bases. For example a domain \( \beta \) can be used to describe 5'-ACTGACGTACATGGA-3', which we can further split into: $$ \overbrace{\underbrace{ACTGAC}_{\beta^{6}} \underbrace{GTACATGGA}_{\beta_{6}}}^{\beta} $$
In this model, the biophysics of the toehold exchange process is established in 3 steps: $$ X(m,n) + S \ \overset{k_{f1}}{ \underset{k_{r(\gamma^n )}}{\rightleftharpoons}} \ I(m,n) \ \overset{k_{b}}{ \underset{k_{b}}{\rightleftharpoons}} \ J(m,n) \ \overset{k_{r(\beta ^m)}}{ \underset{k_{f2}}{\rightleftharpoons}} \ Y + L(m,n) $$ In the 1st step, an invading strand \( X(m,n) \) binds to the substrate complex by toehold \( \bar{\gamma} ^n\). This is called the invading toehold. This reaction forms an Intermediate \( I(m,n) \) which represents all the branch migration states in which \( Y \) is bound to more bases of \( \bar{\beta}_m \) (i.e. \( X(m,n) \) is in the process of displacing \( Y \)). The second intermediate state \( J(m,n) \) conversely represents the states in which \( X(m,n) \) is bound to more bases of \( \beta_m \) (i.e. strand has been more than halfway displaced). After this stage, the toehold \( \beta^m \) dissociates and the forward reaction is complete, with the strand having been displaced.

In this reaction, the rate constants \( k_{f1} \) and \( k_{f2} \) quantify the rates at which \( \gamma^n \) and \( \beta^m \) bind to their respective complements. Winfree assumes that they are equal to each other and denotes the overall hybridization rate \( k_f \) with a value of approximately \(3.5 \times 10^6 M^{-1} s^{-1} \). Note that this constant (along with \( k_b \)) is dependent on various features such as salt concentrations, temperatures and domain length/composition. Despite not being exactly accurate for our purposes, it provides a good approximation for this process.

The first order rates at which the toeholds dissociate are given by: $$ k_{r(\gamma)} = k_{f1} \times \frac{2}{b-m} \times e^{\frac{\Delta G(\gamma^n)}{RT}} $$ where the \( b-m \) term (the length of the \( \beta_m \) domain) ensures that the relative concentrations at equilibrium are accurate and the binding energy is calculated using 'The Thermodynamics of DNA Structural Motifs' (Hicks and Santa Lucia Jr. 2004). The expression for \( k_{r(\gamma^m)} \) is similar to this.

Despite being modelled as a 3-step process, it has been shown (Mills & Yurke 2004) that toehold-mediated strand displacement can be approximated by bimolecular reactions with second-order rate constants. This can be displayed as: $$ X(m,n) + S \quad \overset{k_{(\beta^m, \beta_m, \gamma^n)}}{ \underset{k_{(\gamma^n, \beta_m, \beta^m}}{\rightleftharpoons}} \quad Y + L(m,n) $$ where these rate constants are related to the constants defined previously by: $$k_{(\beta^m, \beta_m, \gamma^n)} \equiv \frac{k_{r(\beta^m)} k_f k_b}{k_{r(\gamma^n)}k_{r(\beta^m)} + k_{r(\gamma^n)}k_b + k_{r(\gamma^m)}k_b} $$ To ensure that these simulations are accurate to within 50%, the concentration of \( X(m,n) \) must be kept below a certain critical concentration which for our purposes is \( 100nM - 1\mu M \). The rate of this reaction also strongly depends on the composition of the toehold. Most estimates are made using mixed teoholds but toeholds can also be assigned as strong (exclusively G/C pairs) and weak (exclusively A/T). This results in a quantifiable difference in binding energy and therefore reaction rates due to the difference in number of Hydrogen bonds.

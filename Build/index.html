Building DNA nanopores

DNA nanopores self-assemble from synthetic single stranded sequences of DNA (oligonucleotides). Oligonucleotides are designed using computer software (cadnano) to fold into specific 3D structures. We designed DNA nanopores as hexameric hollow barrel composed of six double stranded DNA helices. We chose to build this nanopore as this archetypal design has been independently described twice (Langecker et al., 2012; Burns et al., 2013) and has been recently demonstrated to embed in the membranes of liposomes (Burns et al., 2016). Four of the 6 helices are equipped with cholesterol anchors, in order to puncture lipid bilayers and allow to the nanopores to embed across the hydrophobic membranes.

Procedure:

  2. Fill the tank with x1TBE buffer so that it completely submerges the gel.

Make sure to include at least 1 ladder: 0.5 µl of the ladder + 3 µl of gel loading dye + 11.5 µl of water was pipetted into an eppendorf. 10ul of the resultant solution was added to the wells. The stock gel loading dye was at x5 concentration.

  5. Close the tank with its lid and the run the gel for example at 70V for 1 hour. When the bands have travelled a sufficient distance, the power source can be switched off.
  6. Gently lift the gel out of the tank and visualise the SybrGold under UV or blue light.

Building Liposomes

Liposomes (vesicles) are membrane-enclosed spheres that transport molecular cargo within and between cells. We used large unilamellar vesicles (LUVs) with a diameter of 200nm as drug carriers.

The first step of LUV preparation consists of drying phospholipids into glass vials to form “lipid films”. These can be stored at 4°C and taken out for use as needed. List of ingredients:
  • DOPC powder (Avanti Polar Lipids)
  • 25 mg mL⁻¹ DOPE (Avanti Polar Lipids)
  • Chloroform
  • Nitrogen gas
Procedure:
  1. Dissolve the DOPC powder in 3.79 mL of chloroform.
  2. Add dissolved DOPC and DOPE into a vial in a 3:7 DOPE:DOPC ratio and make the solution up to the desired volume with chloroform. Here,In this experiment, 30 uL of DOPE, 70 uL of DOPC and an additional 100 mL of chloroform were used
  3. The vial is dried using nitrogen gas and left in a vacuum oven without any heat, at 400 bar of pressure at least overnight before using.

Liposomes are formed by hydrating the dried phospholipids in an organic solvent. During this step, it is possible to fill the liposomes with hydrophilic molecular cargoes (such as fluorophores and drugs) and embed DNA nanopores onto the membranes. A fresh batch of hydrated liposomes was typically prepared on the same day of experimentation, whereas vials with lipid films were prepared upto 3 weeks in advance and stored at room temperature.


List of ingredients:

  • Methanol (10 µl)
  • DNA nanopores (150 µl, 1 µM in Buffer A)
  • Buffer A (0.3 M KCl, 15 mM TRIS, pH8)
  • Sulforhodamine B (200 mM in Buffer A)

Procedure:

  1. DOPE and DOPC dissolved in chloroform are added to a vial in a 3:7 ratio
  2. 50 µL of 200 mM SRB in buffer A, 150 µL of a 1uM solution of DNA nanopores in buffer A, and 10 µL of methanol are added to the vial. Two separate phases should be visible.
  3. The apolar phase (chloroform) is evaporated in a vacuum rotary evaporator rotating at 200 rpm.

Extrusion is required to convert the multilamellar (i.e. consist of many lipid bilayers) vesicles into unilamellar vesicles. This can improve the efficiency of incorporating fluorophores and nanopores.


List of ingredients:

  • Extruder (Avanti mini extruder kit)
  • Polycarbonate filter (0.2 µm)
  • Filter supports
  • Hydrated vesicles (from step before)

Procedure:

  1. Assemble the extruder following Avanti instructions
  2. With a glass syringe draw the hydrated LUVs solution from the glass vial
  3. Extrude following the extruder-kit instructions. Make sure to exchange fluids between the syringes for an odd number of times, and a minimum of 11.
  4. Collect the extruded solution in a suitably-labeled Eppendorf tube

The extruded solution now contains Large Unilamellar Vesicles (LUVs) filled with fluorophores and DNA nanopores embedded on the membrane. However, many fluorophore molecules remain unencapsulated. To purify LUVs from unincorporated fluorophores size-exclusion chromatography by gel filtration.


List of ingredients:

  • Buffer A (0.3 M KCl, 15 mM TRIS, pH8)
  • Sephadex G-100
  • Sephadex Columns

Procedure:

  1. 0.8 g of sephadex powder is dissolved in 24 mL of buffer A in a 50 mL Falcon tube.
  2. It is left to settle for 3 hours at room temperature or for 1 hour at 65°C in a vacuum oven.
  3. The tip of the column is cut with scissors. A filter is added to the bottom of the column and sephadex is poured on top of it. The number of air bubbles should be minimized. The column is kept open. A waste beaker is placed underneath.
  4. As the sephadex settles, the height of the column should be monitored. Top up sephadex as needed to achieve the appropriate column height.
  5. Once the sephadex solidifies to the point where the column height does not drop any further, even when elution buffer is added, another filter is placed on top of it.
  6. Pre-equilibrate the column with elution buffer.
  7. Pipette a solution of liposomes on top of the column and elute with elution buffer. 1 mL aliquots are collected in Eppendorf™ test tubes.

Building Peptide-Oligonucleotide Conjugates (POCs)

After having succesfully demonstrated the the DNA nanopores open upon detection of the key input and allow the passage of molecular cargoes across the membrane, we aimed to demonstrate the nanopores can be used to sense protein substrates. For this we designed nanoscale Peptide-Oligonucleotide Conjugates (nanoPOCs), covalent constructs that link oligodeoxinucleotides to a synthetic peptide sequences. These materials merge the programmable self-assembly of oligonucleotides with the bioactivity and chemical diversity of polypeptides (MacCullogh et al., 2019). NanoPOCs are composed of peptide sequences flanked by tandem DNA sequences that form defined tertiary structures. The ultimate goal of nanoPOCs is to leverage the unique advantages of each biomolecule into a chimeric molecule that allows DNA nanopores to sense proteins.

NanoPOCs were assembled by separate synthesis and purification of each component (peptides and oligonucleotides) followed by their coupling in solution using the heterobifunctional amine-to-sulfhydryl cross-linker succinimidyl 4-(N-maleimidomethyl)cyclohexane-1-carboxylate (SMCC), following the protocol described by Williams and Chaput, 2011. Oligonucleotides were designed to have extra amine (NH2) groups at their 3’ends (ordered from Integrated DNA Technologies)


List of ingredients:

  • Pure amine-modified oligonucleotides

    • Description Sequence
    Key /5AmMC6/GGGATATATAACGAAAAAAAAGCACGGCTAAGCATGCGACGGAAAAAAAAACGTGCGC
    Anti-key TATATATCCC/3AmMO/

  • Peptide sequences (with 2 terminal cysteines at the N- and C- termini)

    • Description Sequence
    Link1 Cys-Gly-Gly-Lys-Gly-Gly-Cys
    Link2 Cys-Gly-Gly-Gly-Lys-Gly-Gly-Gly-Cys
    Link3 Cys-Gly-Gly-Gly-Gly-Gly-Lys-Gly-Gly-Gly-Gly-Gly-Cys

  • 100 mM KH2OPO4 buffer, pH 7.2
  • SMCC
  • Acetonitrile

Conjugate SMCC to DNA oligonucleotide:

  1. Resuspend the purified oligonucleotide (key) in ultrapure water to reach a 100 μM stock.
  2. Transfer 20 nmoles of the oligonucleotide from step 1 to a new 1.5 mL tube.
  3. Add 134 μL of 100 mM KH2PO4 buffer, pH 7.2 to the tube.
  4. In a separate 1.5 mL tube, dissolve 1 mg of SMCC in 1 mL of acetonitrile for a final concentration of 3 mM.
  5. Transfer 67.0 μL of SMCC (200 nmoles) solution to the oligonucleotide solution prepared in step 4.
  6. Vortex the solution vigorously for 5 seconds. Centrifuge the tube.
  7. Allow the reaction to take place at room temperature for 30 minutes.
  8. Repeat steps 1-7 for a second oligonucleotide (thblock-2) to conjugate with SMCC.
  9. Transfer the contents of the two oligonucleotide-SMCC containing tubes into a new 1.5mL tube.


Conjugate SMCC-oligonucleotide to cysteine-containing peptide:

  1. Resuspend the peptide in water for a final concentration of 1 mg/mL solution.
  2. Add 69.4 μL (100 nmoles) of peptide to the tube in step 9 from the previous procedure.
  3. Vortex the solution for 5 seconds.
  4. Centrifuge the tube briefly.
  5. Let the reaction take place at room temperature for at least 3 hours to obtain crude peptide-oligonucleotide conjugate (POC) material.

Polyacrylamide gels are used to separate smaller fragments of DNA or reveal differences between lanes when the strands differ by a small number of base pairs. Polyacrylamide gels were made without any denaturing agent as described below.


List of ingredients:

  • 1M Tris-HCl pH 8.8
  • Acrylamide/Bisacrylamide (30%/0.8% w/v)
  • 10% (w/v) ammonium persulfate (AP)
  • TEMED
  • BioRad glass plates (one tall, one short per gel)
  • BioRad casting frame and casting stand
  • Isopropanol
  • Comb
  • Deionised water
  • Filter paper

Procedure:

  1. Align the 2 glass plates together and insert them into the casting frame, secure the frame around the plates by clamping them. Suspend the apparatus from the casting stand.
  2. Add some water in between the glass plates and test whether it leaks (it should not). If the test is successful, draw the water out with some filter paper.
  3. Create a gel of the appropriate polyacrylamide strength using the table below as a guide. Note, add 10 µl of TEMED last. TEMED enables the acrylamide to polymerise and if left to sit for a minute, could result in the gel solidifying too much to pipette within the falcon itself. Therefore, it is important to quickly pipette the gel into the glass mould after adding TEMED.

    10% Gel 15% Gel
    Acrylamide/Bis-acrylamide 3.4 ml 5 ml
    Tris-HCl 6.49 ml 4.89 ml
    AP 100 µl 100 µl
    TEMED 10 µl 10 µl

  4. Fill the glass plates with the gel upto a height of about 1 cm beneath (what will later become) the lowest position of the comb.
  5. Remove the bubbles and ensure a straight gel by freely adding isopropanol.
  6. After waiting for about 30 minutes for the gel to solidify, pour off the isopropanol and then dry off any remnants of isopropanol using filter paper.
  7. Now, the stacking gel can be cast. In a 50 ml Falcon, combine 4.275 ml 1 M Tris-HCl, 0.67 ml Acrylamide/Bis-acrylamide, 0.05 ml AP. Lastly, add 5 µl of TEMED. This will result in 5 ml of stacking gel.
  8. Add this up to the top of the glass plates and then insert the comb.
  9. Allow the gel to settle for another 30 minutes before removing the comb.

List of ingredients:
  • Prepared polyacrylamide gel (PAGE)
  • Gel running apparatus (holder, cassette, vertical tank, lid)
  • A power source and cables
  • Gel loading dye
  • Samples to be run
  • PAGE running buffer
  • low and/or high molecular weight ladder(s)
  • Milli-Q (i.e. ultrapure) water
Procedure:
  1. Remove the glass plates from the casting frame and load them into the BioRad running gel apparatus, in accordance with the manufacturer’s instructions. Briefly, keep the short glass facing inwards and slide the plates onto the BioRad holder. The holder can accommodate 2 sets of glass plates (ie 2 gels) at a time. Use a dummy mould (ie glass plates without any gel) if only running 1 gel. Place the loaded holder into the BioRad running cassette and clamp them into position.
  2. Load this into the BioRad tank. Fill the inner chamber to the brim with running buffer. The inner chamber is the portion between the 2 sets of gel plates in the holder/casting cassette. (The outer chamber are the areas between the casting cassette and the walls of the tank)
  3. The running buffer lubricates the comb, making it easier to gently remove the comb without disturbing the rest of the gel.
  4. Stock DNA at 1 µM was diluted using Milli-Q water: 10 µl DNA sample + 2 µl water + 3 µl gel loading dye was pipetted into an eppendorf. 10ul of the resultant solution was loaded into the wells. Make sure to include at least 1 ladder: 0.5 µl of the ladder + 3 µl of gel loading dye + 11.5 µl of water was pipetted into an eppendorf. 10ul of the resultant solution was added to the wells. The stock gel loading dye was at x5 concentration
  5. Load 10 µl of each prepared sample/ladder by inserting the tip of the pipette perpendicularly to the edge of the short glass plate, just above the desired well.
  6. Top the buffer in the inner chamber. Fill the outer chamber in about 600 ml of x1TBE buffer (or x10TBE diluted with Milli-Q water) which is just enough to submerge the bottom of the casting cassette apparatus.
  7. Close the reactor with its lid and run the gel for an appropriate amount of time. For instance, we ran a 25% PAGE at 90V for >1.5 hours. When the gel loading dye bands have travelled a sufficient distance across the gel, the power source can be turned off.
List of ingredients:
  • Prepared polyacrylamide gel (PAGE)
  • ThermoFisher SybrGold (or equivalent)
  • Milli-Q (i.e. ultrapure) water
  • Typhoon FLA 9000 gel imager
Procedure:
  1. The gel is removed from the reactor and from the glass plate mould, carefully ensuring not to break the gel.
  2. SybrGold binds the DNA, allowing it to be visualised. Dissolve 5ul x1000 stock of SybrGold dissolved in 50ml of x1TBE (or x10TBE diluted in Milli-Q water). Immerse the gel in this for about 40 minutes on a shaker.
  3. Place the gel on a UV illuminator for a quick analysis of the position of the DNA bands. For a better quality readout of the position of DNA bands, use an instrument such as the Typhoon FLA 9500TM gel imager. A “SYPRO Ruby” filter on this instrument gave an optimal readout.
